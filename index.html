    05 — Materials

    Quality over quantity, in order of priority

    1. Your coaching's modules and DPPs first. Always. Allen's modules are widely considered the strongest available in India and are worth following separately even if you're not enrolled there.
    2. Reference books second — alongside if you have bandwidth, otherwise after your coaching material is solid.
    3. PYQs are non-negotiable. Skipping them is one of the most common, most expensive mistakes in JEE prep.

    Test series

    Allen's test series is the strongest overall — reportedly calibrated slightly above actual JEE Advanced difficulty — and worth giving even if you're enrolled elsewhere. FIITJEE and MathonGo are solid alternatives. Always give your own institute's series too, and prefer offline test series where possible.

      08 — Olympiads alongside JEE

      Use the overlap. Don't compete with yourself.

      Olympiad foundations built from Class 8–9 give a real edge — conceptual depth and problem-solving intuition that JEE Advanced rewards directly. But starting a full Olympiad batch in Class 11–12, on top of JEE prep, is a common, costly mistake.

      The better strategy in 11th/12th: register for the exams for the exposure and pressure-testing, cover only the syllabus portions that overlap with JEE (especially strong for NSEP/NSEC), and solve Olympiad PYQs as extra problem-solving reps. You get Olympiad-level sharpness without spending JEE's limited clock.

      • NSEJS → INJSO up to Class 10
      • NSEP → INPhO Physics
      • NSEC → INChO Chemistry
      • NSEB → INBO Biology
      • NSEA Astronomy

      Government/HBCSE-recognised Olympiads only — not school-circuit competitions like SilverZone or SOF. For Math Olympiad / ISI / CMI prep specifically, Vedantu Olympiad School (VOS) and Kolkata-based Ramanujan School of Mathematics (RSM) are both well regarded.

      09 — Resources

      Tools and free resources worth bookmarking

      On YouTube "oneshots"

      Oneshots are not a syllabus-completion strategy — sitting through 7–8 hours to "finish" a chapter rarely builds real understanding. "30 days, 30 oneshots, khel khatam" is the cautionary joke for a reason. Use oneshots for backlog clearing and revision; build the syllabus the first time through good books and your coaching's classes. Early, properly done syllabus completion is genuinely valuable — it just has to be properly done.
